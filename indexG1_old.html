Welcome, Families!

You're here! As a caregiver, you play an important role in your child's education. Every day, your child is learning new skills and applying them to everyday life, and that application extends beyond the classroom. You can help your child by encouraging him or her to make connections. Here you will find everything you need to help your child succeed. Every little building block helps, so stack them up!

What Are Foundational Skills?

The Foundational Skills of reading are the building blocks of reading success. This year, your child will learn how letters create words, how words create sentences, and how sentences and pictures come together to create books. Your child will learn about the sounds of the English language and how those sounds are represented in print. The idea that print has meaning is the foundation of all that will come next.

How Print Works

What is a book? What are letters and words, and how do they convey meaning? Concepts of Print are those basic concepts that address how print works, including how language is conveyed through text.


Try This! How can you help your child understand that print has meaning? Try these activities!

  • Visit the public library and explore the children's books. Read the information on the front covers.
  • When reading a book together, follow the words with your finger as you read.
  • While driving in the car, point out any words you see on road signs, billboards, or storefronts. Explain what they mean.
  • When reading a book together, let your child "drive." Allow him or her to hold the book and turn pages as you read.
  • Write and use a grocery list when shopping. Allow your child to help cross out items as you find them.
  • Set a basket of books in your home somewhere, at your child's level. Encourage them to open and explore, even if they can't read.


Let's Read! Help your child understand how to read a book by accessing the trade book I Am a Book. Follow along with the main character, Book, as he learns all about the world of books and reading.

Let's Sing! Play the Concepts of Print See and Sing videos to reinforce what your child is learning in class. What better way to learn than through song?

Listening for Sounds

Did you know there are 42 unique sounds in the English language? Phonological Awareness is the ability to recognize and work with those sounds. What is the first sound in a word? The last sound? Do those words rhyme? Understanding sounds goes hand in hand with reading.

Try This! How can you help your child learn to listen for sounds? Try these activities!

  • While waiting in line at the store, say any word. Have your child say words that rhyme.
  • Make up silly sentences that include words with the same beginning sound.
  • Say the individual sounds in a word, saying each sound separately. Have your child guess the word.
  • When listening to the radio or watching TV, choose a sound to listen for. When children hear a word that includes the sound, they yell it out.
  • At dinner, have children name the food items on their plate and use silverware to tap the number of syllables.
  • Say a sound, such as the /s/ in sat. Take turns saying words that begin with the same sound.


Let's Play! Have your child play Sound Safari to identify sounds in words.
Have your child play Javi's Make and Takeaway Words to practice phonological awareness skills.

Let's Spin! Access the digital spinners to help your child notice and produce sounds.

  • Spin for Sounds: Spin the digital spinner and have your child say the beginning, middle, or ending sound.
  • Spin for Syllables: Spin the digital spinner and have your child clap the number of syllables in the word.
  • Spin and Rhyme: Spin the digital spinner and have your child say more words that rhyme with the word.

Word Work

Through Phonics instruction, children learn that letters represent sounds. Some common words do not follow the Phonics rules, so those words need to be memorized. By learning phonics rules and practicing high-frequency words, children can become independent readers. The end goal is to read fluently and with comprehension.

Try This! How can you help your child learn to read? Try these activities!

  • Have your child practice reading a book to a sibling or stuffed animal.
  • Play a name game! Say somebody's name and ask your child if they know the letter that begins the name. Then come up with other words that start with that letter.
  • When reading a book to your child, allow the child to point out words he or she knows.
  • Use silverware or other household items to form letters. Take turns forming and guessing.
  • Guess the word! Draw blanks on a sheet of paper, each representing a letter. Have children guess letters to find out the word.
  • Write your own foldable decodable. Fold a piece of paper in half and help your child write a story.


Let's Play! Have your child play Spelling Voyage to practice Phonics skills. Have your child play Word Adventure to practice high-frequency words. Print the Game On! board games and create your own game to play with your child.
