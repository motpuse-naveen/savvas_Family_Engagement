Welcome, Families!

You're here! As a caregiver, you play an important role in your child's education. Every day, your child is learning new skills and applying them to everyday life, and that application extends beyond the classroom. You can help your child by encouraging him or her to make connections. Here you will find everything you need to help your child succeed. Every little building block helps, so stack them up!

What Are Foundational Skills?

The Foundational Skills of reading are the building blocks of reading success. This year, your child will learn how letters create words, how words create sentences, and how sentences and pictures come together to create books. Your child will learn about the sounds of the English language and how those sounds are represented in print. The idea that print has meaning is the foundation of all that will come next.

 

How Print Works

What is a book? What are letters and words, and how do they convey meaning? Concepts of Print are those basic concepts that address how print works, including how language is conveyed through text.

Try This! How can you help your child understand that print has meaning? Try these activities!

  • Before reading a book, point out its parts, such as the front cover, back cover, and title page.
  • As you make dinner, search for ingredients in the pantry by reading aloud the words on packages.
  • Visit the public library and explore the children's books, both fiction and nonfiction.
  • At mealtime, set name plates at each table seating and have your child locate his or her spot.
  • Before reading a book, point out the author's name and read any note about the author. Explain that a person wrote the book, and a person drew the pictures!
  • Have your child read you his or her favorite book.

Listening for Sounds

Did you know there are 44 unique sounds in the English language? Phonological Awareness is the ability to recognize and work with those sounds. What is the first sound in a word? The last sound? Do those words rhyme? Understanding sounds goes hand in hand with reading.

Try This! How can you help your child learn to listen for sounds? Try these activities!

  • Say or listen to nursery rhymes. Point out words that rhyme.
  • Say a beginning sound, such as /b/. Then say more words that begin with that sound, and some that do not. Allow the child to be the referee and "buzz" you when you say a word that does not begin with /b/.
  • Play "I-Spy" using the prompt "I spy something that starts with the sound..."
  • Talk like a sloth! Say the individual sounds in a word slowly. Have children blend the sounds together to guess the word.
  • Play hopscotch. Have children say a sentence and hop one time for each word.
  • Everything starts the same! Choose a sound, such as /d/. Name items around the room and change the beginning sound of each to /d/. For example, couch, table, and shirt become douch, dable, and dirt.
Let’s Play!

Let’s Spin!

Access the digital spinners to help your child notice and produce sounds.

  • Spin for Sounds: Spin the digital spinner and have your child say the beginning, middle, or ending sound.
  • Spin for Syllables: Spin the digital spinner and have your child clap the number of syllables in the word.
  • Spin and Rhyme: Spin the digital spinner and have your child say more words that rhyme with the word.

Word Work

Children must first learn to recognize and name the letters of the alphabet. Then, through phonics instruction, children learn that the letters represent sounds. Letters come together to spell words, but some common words do not follow the phonics rules, so those words need to be memorized. By learning phonics rules and practicing high-frequency words, children can become independent readers. The end goal is to read fluently and with comprehension.

Try This! How can you help your child learn to read? Try these activities!

  • At mealtime, use white paper as placemats and encourage children to write and draw.
  • Sing "The Alphabet Song" using silly voices, such as a chipmunk or a whale.
  • Guess the letter! Stand or lie down on the floor and form a letter with your body. Have your child guess. Take turns or play with teams!
  • Play "I am thinking of a letter..." Give clues such as "..that is at the beginning of apple" and "that is in the middle of nap."
  • Choose a letter while you're at the grocery store and go on a letter hunt! Look at signs and packages. Tally up how many you find.
  • Look through the mail and encourage children to point out letters they konw and words they can read.
